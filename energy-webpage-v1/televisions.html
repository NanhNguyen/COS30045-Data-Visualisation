Television Energy Consumption in Australia

Why does TV energy consumption matter?

Many Australian households use televisions daily for entertainment, gaming, and streaming. However, televisions can contribute significantly to household electricity bills, especially when the screen size is large or the TV uses older screen technology.

This page explores energy consumption patterns across different television screen technologies, screen sizes, and power usage to help consumers make more informed purchasing decisions.

Audience

The main audience for this data story is Australian consumers and households who are planning to purchase a new television and want to balance screen quality with energy efficiency.

The audience is likely interested in practical answers such as:

  • Which screen technology is most common?
  • Which technology uses the least power?
  • Does a bigger screen always consume more electricity?

Question 1: What screen technologies are most common in Australia?

To answer this question, the dataset was grouped by Screen_Tech and the number of TV models in each category was counted. A bar chart was chosen because it allows easy comparison between categories and makes the most common technologies immediately visible.

Chart: Frequency of TV Screen Technologies

Insight: LED-based technologies appear most frequently, suggesting that they are the dominant TV type currently available in Australia. Older technologies such as Plasma appear less often, which may indicate they are being phased out.

Question 2: Which screen technology consumes the least power?

This question focuses on energy efficiency. The dataset was grouped by Screen_Tech and the average power consumption was calculated using the Power column.

A bar chart was chosen because it clearly compares average values across multiple groups.

Chart: Average Power Consumption by Screen Technology

Insight: Technologies such as LED and OLED tend to have lower average power consumption compared to older technologies. This suggests that consumers who want to reduce electricity costs should prioritise modern display technologies.

Question 3: What is the relationship between screen size and power use?

To explore this relationship, a scatter plot was chosen because it is the best chart type for examining correlations between two numerical variables.

The x-axis represents Screen Size (inches), and the y-axis represents Power (W). Each dot represents one TV model in the dataset.

Chart: Screen Size vs Power Consumption

Insight: There is a clear positive relationship between screen size and power use. Larger TVs generally consume more electricity, but there are also differences between models, suggesting that energy efficiency depends not only on size but also on technology and model design.

Conclusion and Recommendation

This data story shows that modern screen technologies such as LED and OLED are the most common in Australia and are generally more energy-efficient. The analysis also confirms that larger screen sizes tend to consume more power, which may increase household electricity bills.

Recommendation: If a household wants to reduce energy consumption, they should consider choosing a modern LED/OLED television and avoid unnecessarily large screen sizes unless required.

How were the charts created?

The dataset was explored and aggregated using KNIME. GroupBy and aggregation nodes were used to calculate frequencies and average power consumption. Charts were then generated and exported as images for use in this website.

This approach supports transparency and ensures that the results shown on the website can be reproduced.
